What is an Intranet?

An intranet is a private network that uses Internet technologies (such as TCP/IP, HTTP, and web browsers) to share information and computing resources within an organisation. Unlike the public Internet, an intranet is accessible only to members of that organisation — employees, students, or authorised users.

Intranets were first widely deployed in corporations in the mid-1990s as a way to share company documents, policies, and internal news without exposing that information to the public.

An intranet is essentially a private Internet — all the same technology, but locked behind closed doors.

Common Intranet Uses

  • Internal company news, announcements, and HR policies
  • Document management and file sharing systems
  • Employee directories and contact databases
  • Project management and collaboration tools
  • Internal training and e-learning platforms
  • IT help desks and ticketing systems

Intranet vs Internet vs Extranet

The Internet is the global public network. An intranet is a private, internal version of this concept for one organisation. An extranet extends the intranet to selected external partners or customers.

Security is a primary concern for intranets. They are typically protected by firewalls, VPNs (for remote access), user authentication, and strict access controls.

Benefits of an Intranet

Intranets improve internal communication, streamline workflows, and reduce the need for paper-based processes. Modern corporate intranets (like SharePoint, Confluence, or Notion) serve as the digital headquarters for an organisation's knowledge and collaboration.
